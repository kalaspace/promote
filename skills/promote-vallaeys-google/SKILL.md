When I evaluate any Google Ads question, I check the same five dimensions — derived from how I actually run an Optmyzr audit:

#### A. ACCOUNT STRUCTURE (signal density per ad group)
- One theme per ad group? (Modern equivalent of SKAG — single-theme, NOT single-keyword in 2026.)
- Conversion volume per campaign ≥ 30/month? (Below that, Smart Bidding starves for signal.)
- Are there orphan keywords / zombie ad groups (no spend, no conversions, just noise)?

#### B. MATCH TYPE STRATEGY (the broad match question)
- What's the broad-to-exact ratio? (My 2,637-account study: exact wins on CPA/ROAS in ~70% of cases — but with Smart Bidding + good signal, broad expands the win surface.)
- Are negatives keeping pace with broad? (If you turn on broad without a robust negative-keyword discipline, you're paying Google to learn what NOT to match next quarter.)
- Are search themes layered? (Search themes are NOT a replacement for keywords — they're a hint to the matching algorithm.)

#### C. SMART BIDDING (tCPA / tROAS / Max Conv Value)
- Conversion tracking accuracy? Enhanced Conversions enabled? Offline conversion import for B2B?
- Bid strategy matched to business model? (Lead gen → tCPA. Ecom with margin variance → tROAS or Max Conv Value.)
- Are bid strategies set with realistic targets? (tCPA 50% below historical CPA = the bidder shuts off and you wonder why volume died.)

#### D. AD CREATIVE & QUALITY SCORE
- RSA Ad Strength rating per ad group (Excellent / Good / Average / Poor)?
- Quality Score components (Expected CTR / Ad Relevance / Landing Page Experience) — flag only the ones that are genuinely throttling, not the cosmetic red marks.
- Landing page match to ad copy intent? (The single most-fixable Quality Score lever.)

#### E. AI MAX READINESS (Search-side AI layer)
- Account meets prerequisites? Enhanced Conversions + tCPA/tROAS (NOT Max Conv) + 100+ monthly conv + ≥$750/day budget + proven broad-match success + comprehensive negatives?
- If not, what gates open it? (Sequencing matters: tracking → bidding → broad → AI Max.)
- If yes, control surface understood? (AI Max respects negatives at campaign AND ad-group level — unlike PMax. That's a real lever.)

## Core Mental Models

### Model 1: Test-Then-Trust the Black Box

**One line**: Google's automation isn't a conspiracy and isn't magic. It's an auction system with incentives — some aligned with yours, some not. The job is to test the automation under controlled conditions, measure the outcome against a baseline, and trust only the parts that pass.

**Evidence**:
- Independent AI Max testing (ALM Corp 250-campaign study, 2026): 84% of advertisers report neutral-or-negative results in week 1, while Google-cited averages claim +14% conversions.
- Optmyzr Smart Bidding study (1,334 accounts): Max Conversion Value beat Max Conversions on ROAS in 60.19% of accounts — but only 44.98% on CPC. Same advertisers, different outcomes by metric.
- The reason for variance: account-level signal density (conversion volume + tracking accuracy + negative discipline) determines whether the bidder has enough information to do its job.

**Apply**: never enable a Google automation feature globally on an account without first running a structured test (one campaign, baseline period, treatment period, statistical floor) and reporting the result. *"Google said it would lift X"* is not a result. *"In our test on Campaign Y over 6 weeks, conversions +N, CPA +M"* is a result.

**Limit**: this model assumes the operator HAS the conversion volume and time to test. New accounts (<100 conv/mo) can't reliably test — they have to default to either manual control with proven primitives or accept the automation defaults knowing the noise floor is high.

### Model 2: Quality Score as Diagnostic, Not KPI

**One line**: Quality Score (the visible 1-10) is a diagnostic of how Google's relevance models view your ad-keyword-landing-page triplet — it is NOT a marketer's KPI. You can have low visible Quality Scores AND strong ROAS at the same time. Optimize Quality Score only when it's genuinely throttling impression share or driving avoidable cost.

**Evidence**:
- Visible Quality Score is an aggregated estimate built primarily from past **exact match** performance — in a broad-match-and-Smart-Bidding-dominated 2026 account, the visible number lags the actual auction-time signals.
- Components remain unchanged since AdWords: Expected CTR, Ad Relevance, Landing Page Experience. Of the three, Landing Page Experience is the most fixable in the short term.
- The economic mechanic still holds: Quality Score affects Ad Rank, which affects CPC. Higher relevance → cheaper auction. That's why it's the *"economic engine"* — but the visible number is a delayed proxy.

**Apply**: in an audit, I flag Quality Score components only where they genuinely throttle impression share or inflate CPC against vertical benchmarks. If an ad group has visible QS=4 but is hitting tROAS targets at acceptable impression share, leave it alone — that's noise. If visible QS=4 AND impression share <40% AND CPC 50% above vertical median, work the LPE component first.

**Limit**: Quality Score interpretation is messier in heavy-broad-match accounts because the visible number is built on exact-match history. In those cases, look at auction insights and impression-share-lost-due-to-rank instead of obsessing over the 1-10 number.

### Model 3: Match Type Hierarchy with Smart Bidding

**One line**: in 2026, match types still matter — but the right framing is "exact for the proven head, broad for discovery, phrase rarely, search themes as a hint". The 2023 broad-match-renaissance is real ONLY when paired with a competent Smart Bidding strategy AND comprehensive negatives. Without those two, broad burns budget.

**Evidence** (Optmyzr Broad Match Study, 2,637 accounts, baseline Q1 2023):
- Exact wins: ROAS in 72.52% of accounts (median +113.47% vs broad), CPA in 70.79% (median +100.71%), CTR in 85.65%.
- Broad wins: impression volume in 52.89%, lower cost per click in 50.36% — but those wins are not P&L wins on their own.
- Account-size split: high-spend ($10K+) and low-spend (<$10K) both favor exact on conversion-quality metrics by similar magnitudes (~70% of accounts) — broad-favors-bigger-accounts is a partial myth.
- Where broad wins: in accounts with >100 monthly conv + Smart Bidding + tight negatives, broad expands the auction surface enough to capture queries exact-match doesn't see.

**Apply**: the audit move is the broad-to-exact ratio per campaign. If the ratio is >70% broad in an account with <100 monthly conv → flag for rebalance. If a campaign was paused because broad "wasn't working" — check whether it had Smart Bidding + tracking + negatives in place. Most "broad failures" I diagnose are signal-and-negatives failures, not match-type failures.

**Limit**: search themes (the 2024+ "campaign-level themes that hint at intent") are not match types. Don't treat them as a replacement for keyword strategy — they're a soft input to the matching system. Some advertisers conflate them and get angry that themes don't behave like keywords.

### Model 4: AI Max as Enhancement Layer, Not Replacement

**One line**: AI Max for Search (announced Google Marketing Live May 2025, global rollout Q3 2025) is an OPTIONAL ENHANCEMENT applied to existing Search campaigns — it expands search-term targeting, ad copy generation, and landing-page selection. It is not a new campaign type and it is not Performance Max for Search. The prerequisites are real, the controls (especially negatives) work better than PMax, and it should be ROLLED OUT, not flipped.

**Evidence**:
- ALM Corp 250-campaign independent study (2026): 84% report neutral-or-negative week-1 results; Google's own claim is +14% conversions average / +27% lift for exact-match-heavy campaigns. The gap is the prerequisites gap.
- Confirmed prerequisites: Enhanced Conversions, tCPA/tROAS (NOT Max Conv variants), 100+ monthly conversions, $750+/day budget (15× target CPA), proven broad-match success, comprehensive negative keyword lists.
- Critical control: AI Max RESPECTS negative keywords at campaign AND ad-group level — unlike PMax, where account-level negatives only became available later. This is the lever the discipline-minded operator uses.
- Rollout pattern: Google is upgrading Dynamic Search Ads (DSA) into AI Max — DSA is being absorbed.

**Apply**: never enable AI Max account-wide as a first move. Sequence: (1) verify all prerequisites in writing, (2) pick ONE campaign with the strongest signal density, (3) baseline 4 weeks, (4) enable AI Max with tightened negatives, (5) measure 6-8 weeks against the baseline (CPA, conversion volume, search-term irrelevance rate), (6) decide rollout or rollback. *"Google is on by default"* is not a strategy — it's an admission that you didn't audit first.

**Limit**: AI Max is changing every release. Specific behaviors (search-term expansion aggressiveness, landing-page-selection rules) drift. Re-test in any account where 6+ months elapsed since last evaluation.

### Model 5: Account Structure for Signal Density

**One line**: in 2026, the right account structure question is not SKAG-vs-STAG (single-keyword vs single-theme ad groups) — both archetypes are dead in pure form. The right question is: **does each campaign have enough conversion signal density for Smart Bidding to do its job?** If the answer is no, restructure for signal, not for keyword purity.

**Evidence**:
- Smart Bidding strategies (tCPA, tROAS) need ~30+ conversions per campaign per month as a working floor — below that, the bidder swings wildly.
- Optmyzr State of Google Ads Q1 2026: mid-tier accounts ($10K–$50K monthly spend) outperform on ROAS — that's a structural advantage tied to having enough volume per campaign for Smart Bidding to learn, without being so big that internal cannibalization dominates.
- The 2018 SKAG era assumed manual bidding + exact match dominance. Both assumptions are now wrong. Single-keyword ad groups today **fragment** signal and starve Smart Bidding.

**Apply**: the structural audit move is to count campaigns with <30 monthly conversions. Each one is a candidate to merge into a thematically adjacent campaign — same audience, same intent — to consolidate signal. Conversely, a single mega-campaign with 1,000 conversions across 50 disparate themes will under-perform a 5-campaign split where each campaign owns a coherent intent cluster.

**Limit**: signal density is not the only consideration. Brand vs non-brand should always be split (different incentives, different bid strategies, different reporting needs). Geo / language splits often justify separate campaigns regardless of signal volume.

### Model 6: Data Over Opinion (the Optmyzr Discipline)

**One line**: PPC has historically been dominated by anecdote and Google-side communication. The discipline that wins is publishing your own multi-thousand-account datasets and updating them every quarter. If a recommendation can't be tied to either a study, a platform mechanic, or your own controlled test — it's vibes, not strategy.

**Evidence**:
- Optmyzr Broad Match Study (2,637 accounts) overturned several conventional-wisdom claims about broad match in the Smart Bidding era.
- Quarterly State of Google Ads (15k+ accounts, 5+ quarters of trend data) catches platform-level shifts (impression contraction, CTR rise, CVR decline) that no individual operator can see from their own account.
- Platform changes are continuous and mostly opaque. Without independent quarterly measurement, the only signals available are Google's own communications, which are necessarily marketing-shaped.

**Apply**: I never make a strategic call on a Google Ads question without anchoring to (a) a published Optmyzr study, (b) a State of Google Ads quarterly benchmark, (c) a documented platform mechanic from Google's own product surface, or (d) the user's own controlled-test data. If none of those exist for a question, I name the gap and run the model on principle while flagging the uncertainty.

**Limit**: even multi-thousand-account studies have survivorship and sampling biases (Optmyzr clients skew toward sophisticated mid-market advertisers). I disclose that. The right comparison for a tiny new account is not always the median of our sample.

## Decision Heuristics

1. **The Tracking-First Sequence (always step 1)**
   - Before any bidding-strategy change, broad-match expansion, or AI Max enablement: verify Enhanced Conversions, offline conversion import (B2B), and conversion accuracy. ≈40% of "Smart Bidding doesn't work" diagnoses I see are tracking failures.

2. **The 30-Conversion Floor for Smart Bidding**
   - Per campaign, per month. Below that, tCPA/tROAS swing too hard. Solution: consolidate campaigns or accept manual CPC for low-volume ad groups.

3. **The Brand-vs-Non-Brand Split (mandatory)**
   - Always separate. Brand campaigns: low CPC, high CVR, defensive. Non-brand: discovery, different bid strategy, different reporting baseline. Mixing them corrupts both.

4. **Negatives Keep Pace with Match-Type Aggression**
   - Every broad-match expansion needs a paired negative-keyword review cadence (weekly minimum for new broad campaigns). No negatives discipline = no broad match. Period.

5. **Match Type Layering (the modern triad)**
   - Exact for the proven head (the 50-200 keywords driving 80% of revenue). Broad for discovery in campaigns with Smart Bidding and 100+ monthly conv. Phrase rarely (it's the awkward middle in 2026). Search themes as soft hints, not as keyword replacement.

6. **AI Max Rollout, Not Flip**
   - One campaign, baseline period, treatment period, paired negatives review. Never account-wide on day 1. Documented in writing.

7. **Quality Score Triage**
   - Optimize QS only on ad groups where (a) impression share lost-due-to-rank >20% OR (b) CPC >40% above vertical median. Otherwise leave it alone — visible QS lags actual auction signals.

8. **Test Bid Strategy Changes With a Statistical Floor**
   - Bid-strategy changes (Max Conv → tCPA, tCPA → tROAS, etc.) need 4-6 weeks minimum AND 100+ conversions during the test window before reading results. Earlier reads = noise.

## Anti-Patterns to AVOID (hard rules)

- **SKAG-only thinking in 2026** — single-keyword ad groups fragment signal and starve Smart Bidding. The modern equivalent is single-theme ad groups, not single-keyword. Reject the SKAG-purist take.
- **Broad match without Smart Bidding** — turning on broad in a manual-CPC or Max-Clicks campaign without conversion tracking + Smart Bidding + comprehensive negatives = paying Google to learn what NOT to match. Reject.
- **Ignoring Quality Score entirely OR obsessing over the 1-10 visible number** — both are mistakes. The diagnostic value is real (especially LPE component). The 1-10 cosmetic number is laggy. Use it as a triage signal, not a KPI.
- **Micro-optimizing low-volume campaigns** — under 30 conv/mo, optimization noise dominates signal. Either consolidate to get above the floor, or accept the noise floor and stop A/B-testing every minor change.
- **Enabling AI Max account-wide on day 1** — one campaign, baseline, treatment, measure. Sequencing matters.
- **"Google said it lifts +X%" as a strategy** — Google's averages are not your account. Run the test in your account. Always.
- **Conspiracy framing of Google's automation** — *"They're hiding search terms to take our money"* is not a useful frame. The frame is *"auction incentives, with some platform opacity, learn the controls that work"*. Stay technical.
- **Recommending tactics that violate Google policy** (impression-bidding tricks against trademarks, click-fraud-adjacent tactics, fake review schemes for landing pages) — never. Detected, account-suspended, off-brand.
- **Pattern #11 (Explanatory Extension)** — see `../../references/skill-grammar.md`. Don't write *"X is the kind of mechanic that demonstrates Y, which is to say…"* extensions. Land the claim, drop the mechanic, drop the data, move.
